# Word Parsing Ruleset: The Perception Engine

## What This Document Is

This is the ruleset for Whelm's word extraction system — the intelligence that determines what "emotionally charged residue" remains when a user's narrative dissolves in the brain dump void.

This system is the bridge between raw overwhelm and the word portrait. Its quality determines whether the mirror reflects something true or something noise.

---

## Core Philosophy

### What We're Listening For

We are not scanning for "negative words." We are listening for **the places where someone's language reveals something they may not yet see** — judgment disguised as description, borrowed words disguised as self-knowledge, patterns disguised as facts.

The goal is not keyword extraction. It is **emotional signal detection**.

### The Therapeutic Frame

In therapy, a skilled practitioner notices:
- The word a client keeps returning to
- The adjective that doesn't quite fit
- The phrase that sounds like someone else's voice
- The moment language shifts from describing to judging
- The places where one feeling is hiding behind another
- The spot where "I feel" becomes "I am"

This system attempts to encode that kind of listening.

### What We Surface vs. What We Don't

**Surface:** Words and phrases that carry emotional weight, reveal judgment, encode identity, signal borrowed beliefs, or mark overwhelm patterns.

**Don't surface:** Neutral narrative, logistical details, proper nouns, factual descriptions, connective language without emotional charge.

### The Humility Principle

This system will be wrong sometimes. It will miss things. It will flag things that don't resonate. That's why the word portrait includes user agency — the ability to remove words that don't land and highlight ones the system missed. The system proposes; the user decides.

## Signal Categories

### CATEGORY 1: Self-Judgment Language

**What it is:** Words and phrases where the speaker evaluates, labels, or passes verdict on themselves. This is the most common and most therapeutically significant category.

**Why it matters:** Self-judgment language often contains borrowed beliefs, internalized criticism, and collapsed complexity. When someone says "I'm toxic," they've compressed a complex relational dynamic into a single identity label — and it's almost never originally their word.

#### 1A: Direct Self-Labels (Identity Statements)

Pattern: `I am [charged noun/adjective]` or `I'm [charged noun/adjective]`

**Flag the label word, not "I am."**

Examples and what to catch:
- "I'm **broken**" → broken
- "I'm such a **mess**" → mess
- "I'm **toxic**" → toxic
- "I'm a **burden**" → burden
- "I'm **damaged**" → damaged
- "I'm **worthless**" → worthless
- "I'm **weak**" → weak
- "I'm a complete **failure**" → failure
- "I'm **unlovable**" → unlovable
- "I'm **too much**" → too much (compound — see Category 2)
- "I'm **not enough**" → not enough (compound — see Category 7)
- "I'm a **bad** person" → bad
- "I'm **selfish**" → selfish
- "I'm **crazy**" → crazy
- "I'm so **dramatic**" → dramatic
- "I'm **needy**" → needy
- "I'm a **fraud**" → fraud
- "I'm **ungrateful**" → ungrateful

**Context sensitivity:** "I'm tired" is different from "I'm broken." Physical states (tired, hungry, cold) are generally lower signal unless combined with judgment ("I'm always tired" — see absolutist language). The signal increases when the label implies a permanent characteristic rather than a temporary state.

**Important nuance:** Watch for identity statements disguised as descriptions:
- "I'm the kind of person who **ruins** things" → ruins
- "I'm someone who **can't** be trusted" → can't be trusted
- "I'm not the type to **deserve** that" → deserve

#### 1B: Verbs Used as Identity

Pattern: `I keep [verb]ing` / `I always [verb]` / `I'm [verb]ing everything`

**What to catch:** The verb that has been elevated from an action to a character trait.

Examples:
- "I keep **sabotaging** everything" → sabotaging
- "I'm **destroying** my relationship" → destroying
- "I keep **pushing** people away" → pushing away
- "I'm **ruining** this" → ruining
- "I **wrecked** it" → wrecked
- "I keep **failing** at this" → failing
- "I'm **abandoning** my responsibilities" → abandoning
- "I keep **repeating** the same mistakes" → repeating
- "I'm **spiraling**" → spiraling (note: this is also a somatic/metaphor word)
- "I keep **betraying** myself" → betraying

**Context sensitivity:** "I keep forgetting to buy milk" is logistical. "I keep forgetting what matters" is charged. The signal is in what's being verbed, not the verb pattern alone.

#### 1C: Nouns Used as Metaphor for Self

Pattern: `I'm a [metaphorical noun]` / `I feel like a [noun]`

**What to catch:** The metaphorical noun — it reveals how the person sees themselves in that moment.

Examples:
- "I'm a **shell** of myself" → shell
- "I feel like a **ghost**" → ghost
- "I'm a **doormat**" → doormat
- "I feel like a **machine**" → machine
- "I'm just a **placeholder**" → placeholder
- "I feel like a **child**" → child (high signal — may indicate parts/IFS work)
- "I'm a walking **disaster**" → disaster
- "I feel like an **imposter**" → imposter
- "I'm a **black hole**" → black hole
- "I feel like an **object**" → object

**Therapeutic significance:** Metaphorical self-description often bypasses cognitive defenses. "I feel like a ghost" contains more embodied truth than "I feel disconnected." The metaphor IS the signal.

### CATEGORY 2: Intensity Markers ("Too" / "So" / "Very" Patterns)

**What it is:** Qualifier words that reveal a judgment threshold has been exceeded — someone (often not the speaker) has decided there's an acceptable amount of this quality, and the speaker has been told they surpass it.

**Why it matters:** "Too ___" is almost always echoed language. Someone, somewhere, told this person they were too much of something. The qualifier reveals the judgment; the adjective reveals the wound.

#### 2A: "Too + [Adjective/Adverb]"

Pattern: `too [adjective]`

**Flag the entire compound: "too [word]"** — the "too" is part of the signal because it reveals the judgment frame.

Examples:
- "I'm **too sensitive**" → too sensitive
- "I'm **too emotional**" → too emotional
- "I'm **too much**" → too much
- "I care **too deeply**" → too deeply
- "I'm **too intense**" → too intense
- "I'm **too needy**" → too needy
- "I think **too much**" → too much
- "I'm **too old** for this" → too old
- "I'm **too broken** to fix" → too broken
- "I trust **too easily**" → too easily
- "I'm **too dependent**" → too dependent
- "I'm **too picky**" → too picky
- "I give **too much**" → too much

**Therapeutic significance:** The word after "too" almost always names a quality the person has been shamed for. It's frequently something that was once a strength or a natural way of being that was pathologized by someone else. "Too sensitive" often means "your sensitivity was inconvenient for someone."

**Context sensitivity:** "It's too hot outside" is not a signal. The pattern requires self-reference proximity or emotional context. "This is taking too long" — low signal. "I'm taking too long to get over this" — high signal (combines "too" with self-judgment about pace of healing).

#### 2B: "So + [Adjective]" (Intensifier)

Pattern: `so [adjective]` in emotional context

**Flag the adjective. "So" is the intensifier that signals the adjective carries extra weight.**

Examples:
- "I'm **so tired** of this" → tired (the "so" elevates "tired" from physical to existential)
- "I feel **so stupid**" → stupid
- "I'm **so angry** at myself" → angry
- "This is **so overwhelming**" → overwhelming
- "I feel **so alone**" → alone
- "I'm **so lost**" → lost
- "I feel **so stuck**" → stuck

**Context sensitivity:** "So" is extremely common in casual speech. Only flag when it intensifies emotional or self-evaluative content. "That movie was so good" — no signal. "I feel so good about myself" — low signal (positive self-assessment; could flag for the word portrait but with different weight). "I feel so ashamed" — high signal.

#### 2C: "Really" / "Extremely" / "Incredibly" + Emotional Content

Same logic as "so" — these amplify the word they modify, but only flag in emotional context.

- "I'm **really struggling**" → struggling
- "It's **incredibly hard**" → hard
- "I feel **deeply ashamed**" → ashamed

### CATEGORY 3: Absolutist Language (Overgeneralization)

**What it is:** Words that collapse specific experiences into universal, permanent truths. In Whelm's vocabulary, this is "growing bigger than the moment."

**Why it matters:** Absolutist language is a hallmark of cognitive distortion (CBT frame) and overwhelm-state thinking. When someone says "I always do this," they've taken a moment and made it a life sentence. The absolutist word is the mechanism of the expansion.

#### 3A: Temporal Absolutism

Pattern: `always` / `never` / `every time` / `forever`

**Flag the absolutist word AND what it modifies.**

Examples:
- "I **always** mess things up" → always + mess up
- "I **never** get this right" → never + get right
- "**Every time** I try, it falls apart" → every time + falls apart
- "This is **never** going to change" → never going to change
- "I'll **always** be like this" → always be like this
- "I **always** end up alone" → always + alone
- "**Nothing ever** works" → nothing ever

**Context sensitivity:** "I always take the subway" is factual/habitual — no signal. "I always end up hurting people" is absolutist self-judgment — high signal. The test: does the absolutist word transform a specific event into a permanent identity?

#### 3B: Scope Absolutism

Pattern: `everything` / `nothing` / `everyone` / `no one` / `all` / `none`

Examples:
- "**Everything** is falling apart" → everything
- "**Nothing** I do matters" → nothing + matters
- "**Everyone** can see I'm failing" → everyone + failing
- "**No one** understands" → no one + understands
- "I've ruined **everything**" → ruined + everything
- "**None** of this matters" → none + matters
- "**Everyone else** can handle this" → everyone else + handle

**Therapeutic significance:** Scope absolutism often signals a collapse of nuance — the overwhelm has made the person unable to differentiate between what's actually affected and what isn't. The word itself ("everything," "nothing") IS the overwhelm talking.

#### 3C: Permanence Language

Pattern: Words that cast current states as unchangeable.

- "I'll **never** be able to..." → never
- "This is just **who I am**" → who I am (resignation)
- "It's **too late**" → too late
- "I **can't change**" → can't change
- "I'm **stuck** like this" → stuck
- "There's **no point**" → no point

### CATEGORY 4: Obligation & Externalized Authority ("Should" Patterns)

**What it is:** Language that reveals the speaker is operating from external rules rather than internal truth. Someone else's "should" has been swallowed and now speaks as if it's the user's own voice.

**Why it matters:** "Should" is one of the most therapeutically significant words in the English language. In Albert Ellis's framework, "shoulding" is a primary mechanism of emotional disturbance. In IFS terms, "should" often speaks from a protector part. In Whelm's terms, it's frequently an echo.

#### 4A: Direct Obligation Words

Pattern: `should` / `shouldn't` / `supposed to` / `have to` / `need to` / `must` / `ought to`

**Flag the obligation word AND what follows it.**

Examples:
- "I **should** be over this by now" → should + over this by now
- "I **shouldn't** feel this way" → shouldn't + feel this way
- "I'm **supposed to** be stronger" → supposed to + stronger
- "I **have to** keep it together" → have to + keep it together
- "I **need to** stop being so sensitive" → need to + stop being sensitive
- "I **must** be doing something wrong" → must + doing something wrong
- "I **ought to** be grateful" → ought to + grateful
- "I **should** know better" → should + know better
- "I **shouldn't** need help with this" → shouldn't + need help

**Critical nuance — "should" vs. practical obligation:** "I should pick up groceries" is a to-do, not a wound. "I should be a better mother" is a judgment. The signal is in what domain the "should" operates: practical/logistical = low signal. Identity/emotional/relational = high signal.

**Detection heuristic:** If you can ask "says who?" after the statement and the answer isn't obvious, it's likely an internalized external voice.

#### 4B: Implicit Obligation (Without "Should")

Sometimes obligation hides in other constructions:

- "A good partner would..." → good + standard being imposed
- "Normal people don't..." → normal + exclusion from normal
- "Everyone else manages to..." → everyone else + comparison
- "I used to be able to..." → used to + loss of capability
- "By now I should have..." → by now + timeline pressure
- "At my age..." → age + timeline pressure
- "After everything they've done for me..." → guilt-driven obligation

#### 4C: The "Why Can't I Just" Pattern

Pattern: `why can't I just [verb]`

This is meta-frustration — frustration about being frustrated. It's a should wrapped in a question.

- "**Why can't I just** let this go" → let go (the thing they can't release)
- "**Why can't I just** be normal" → normal (the standard they're failing)
- "**Why can't I just** get over it" → get over it (the timeline pressure)
- "**Why can't I just** be happy" → happy (what they believe they should be)

**Flag both the pattern and the content word.** "Why can't I just" is itself a signal of meta-anxiety.

### CATEGORY 5: Minimizing & Dismissal Language

**What it is:** Words that shrink, trivialize, or pre-emptively defend against the speaker's own experience. The speaker is discounting themselves before anyone else can.

**Why it matters:** Minimizing is a self-protective strategy, often learned from having emotions dismissed. When someone says "I'm just being dramatic," they're performing the dismissal they expect from others. The minimizing word is the fingerprint of that expectation.

#### 5A: "Just" as Minimizer

Pattern: `just [self-deprecating statement]` / `I'm just [dismissive word]`

**"Just" is one of the most important words to track in context.**

Examples:
- "I'm **just** being **dramatic**" → just + dramatic
- "It's **just** me **overreacting**" → just + overreacting
- "I'm **just** being **silly**" → just + silly
- "It's **just** a **small thing**" → just + small (minimizing the trigger)
- "I'm **just** **tired**" → just + tired (when clearly more is happening)
- "I **just** need to get over it" → just + get over it

**Context sensitivity:** "I just got home" = no signal. "I just need to stop being like this" = high signal. The minimizing "just" appears when it's placed before a self-dismissal or when it shrinks the significance of something that clearly matters.

#### 5B: Self-Dismissal Phrases

These are pre-emptive defenses against being taken seriously:

- "I **know** this is **stupid** but..." → stupid (the user calling their own concern stupid)
- "It's **not a big deal**" → not a big deal (usually IS a big deal)
- "I'm **probably overreacting**" → overreacting
- "I **know** it **sounds crazy**" → crazy
- "I'm sure it's **nothing**" → nothing
- "**Whatever**, it doesn't **matter**" → whatever + doesn't matter
- "I don't even know **why** I'm **upset**" → why + upset (delegitimizing their own emotion)
- "I'm being **ridiculous**" → ridiculous
- "**Forget** it, it's **fine**" → forget it + fine

**Therapeutic significance:** "It's fine" is one of the most loaded phrases in emotional communication. In overwhelm text, "fine" almost always means "I've decided my feelings don't deserve space." Flag it.

#### 5C: "Only" as Self-Shrinking

- "I'm the **only** one who can't handle this" → only + can't handle
- "It **only** bothers me" → only
- "I'm **only** like this because..." → only (minimizing the cause)

### CATEGORY 6: Metaphorical & Embodied Language

**What it is:** Non-literal language that encodes emotional experience through physical, spatial, or elemental metaphors. This is often where the body speaks through words.

**Why it matters:** People describe internal states using physical metaphors because the body's experience is often more accurate than cognitive labels. "I'm drowning" is not a clinical term — it's a somatic truth expressed through language. These words are the bridge between the brain dump and the body scan.

#### 6A: Spatial/Movement Metaphors

These describe the felt sense of being somewhere or moving (or unable to move):

- "**stuck**" → high signal — one of the most common overwhelm words
- "**trapped**" → containment without agency
- "**lost**" → disorientation, lack of direction
- "**falling**" / "**falling apart**" → loss of structure
- "**spinning**" → overwhelm itself, too much input
- "**spiraling**" → downward movement, loss of control
- "**sinking**" → being pulled down
- "**floating**" → dissociation, untethered
- "**scattered**" → fragmentation
- "**unmoored**" → disconnection from anchor
- "**adrift**" → no direction
- "**going in circles**" → repetition without progress
- "**behind**" → (CONTEXT DEPENDENT — see rules below)
- "**upside down**" → disorientation
- "**off balance**" → destabilized
- "**running**" / "**running away**" → avoidance
- "**frozen**" → immobility, shutdown
- "**paralyzed**" → inability to act

**Critical context rule for "behind":** "Behind" alone is not a signal. "The store is behind the bank" = no signal. But "behind" becomes a signal when:
- It's self-referential: "I feel behind **in my life**"
- It implies a standard: "I'm behind where I should be"
- It's paired with comparison: "everyone else is ahead and I'm behind"
- It implies a timeline: "I'm so far behind"

The same rule applies to: "ahead," "below," "beneath," "above" — spatial words gain signal from self-referential + evaluative context.

#### 6B: Weight/Pressure Metaphors

- "**heavy**" → carrying too much
- "**crushing**" / "**crushed**" → overwhelmed by weight
- "**carrying** so much" → burden
- "**weighed down**" → unable to rise
- "**pressure**" → external force
- "**suffocating**" → can't breathe, too much closeness or demand
- "**buried**" → hidden under obligations/feelings
- "**sinking**" → going under

#### 6C: Water/Fluid Metaphors

Especially relevant given Whelm's water vocabulary:

- "**drowning**" → overwhelm, too much input
- "**flooding**" / "**flooded**" → emotions exceeding capacity
- "**waves**" → recurring emotional surges
- "**underwater**" → submerged, can't surface
- "**overflowing**" → too full to contain
- "**dry**" / "**dried up**" → depleted, empty
- "**turbulent**" → internal chaos

#### 6D: Container Metaphors

- "**empty**" → depleted, nothing left
- "**hollow**" → absence of substance
- "**overflowing**" → too full
- "**shattered**" / "**broken**" → structural failure
- "**cracked**" → partial failure, about to break
- "**walls**" → self-protection, isolation
- "**shell**" → exterior with nothing inside
- "**armor**" → self-protection
- "**raw**" → exposed, no protection
- "**exposed**" → vulnerability without safety

#### 6E: Temperature/Energy Metaphors

- "**numb**" → shutdown, dissociation
- "**burning**" / "**on fire**" → rage, urgency, inflammation
- "**cold**" → emotional distance, withdrawal
- "**frozen**" → immobility, fear
- "**boiling**" → anger approaching eruption
- "**drained**" → energy depleted
- "**exhausted**" → (higher signal than "tired" in emotional context)
- "**dead inside**" → (very high signal — dissociation, depression)
- "**electric**" / "**buzzing**" → anxiety, hyperactivation

#### 6F: Darkness/Light Metaphors

- "**dark**" / "**darkness**" → depression, hopelessness
- "**fog**" / "**foggy**" → confusion, dissociation
- "**cloudy**" → unclear thinking
- "**dim**" → diminished
- "**shadow**" → hidden parts, Jung's shadow

### CATEGORY 7: Comparative & Relational Positioning

**What it is:** Language that defines the self in relation to others, usually unfavorably. The speaker measures themselves against a standard and comes up short.

**Why it matters:** Comparison language reveals internalized hierarchies. "Not enough" implies a threshold set by someone or something outside the self. "Less than" implies a ranking. These words often mask grief — grief for the self they think they should be.

#### 7A: "Not Enough" Patterns

Pattern: `not [adjective] enough`

**Flag the entire compound.**

- "**Not good enough**" → not good enough
- "**Not strong enough**" → not strong enough
- "**Not smart enough**" → not smart enough
- "**Not trying hard enough**" → not trying hard enough
- "**Not lovable enough**" → not lovable enough
- "**Not enough**" (standalone) → not enough

**Also catch the inverse:** "Too much" and "not enough" are the same wound from different directions. "Too sensitive" = "not tough enough." They're mirror images.

#### 7B: Direct Comparison

- "**Unlike** everyone else..." → unlike
- "**Compared to** them..." → compared to
- "**Less than**" → less than
- "Everyone **else** can..." → else (marker of self-exclusion)
- "**Other** people manage to..." → other people (marker of self-exclusion)
- "They make it look **easy**" → easy (the standard being failed)

#### 7C: Self-Exclusion Language

Words that place the speaker outside a group they believe they should belong to:

- "**Normal** people don't feel this way" → normal
- "I'm the **only** one" → only
- "**Everyone else** has it figured out" → everyone else
- "I don't **belong**" → belong
- "I'm on the **outside**" → outside
- "I'm **different**" → different (when said with pain, not pride)
- "I don't **fit**" → fit

### CATEGORY 8: Echoed & Borrowed Language

**What it is:** Words that don't originate from the speaker's own felt experience but have been internalized from others — parents, partners, culture, media, previous therapists. The speaker uses these words about themselves as if they're self-knowledge, but they're actually someone else's assessment.

**Why it matters:** This is one of the most therapeutically significant categories. In Whelm's framework, echoed language is a primary target for investigation. When "toxic" gets traced back to an ex's accusation, the user discovers that the word they've been using to judge themselves was never theirs. That's the pivot from investigation to authorship.

#### 8A: Clinical/Diagnostic Language Used About Self

When a non-clinician uses clinical terminology about themselves, it's almost always borrowed:

- "I'm so **codependent**" → codependent
- "I'm being **narcissistic**" → narcissistic
- "That was a **trauma response**" → trauma response
- "I'm **dissociating**" → dissociating (when used as self-diagnosis)
- "I'm **triggered**" → triggered (when used dismissively about self)
- "My **attachment style** makes me..." → attachment style
- "I have an **anxious** attachment" → anxious (when used as identity)
- "I'm being **avoidant**" → avoidant
- "That's my **abandonment** wound" → abandonment
- "I'm so **dysregulated**" → dysregulated
- "I'm being **hypervigilant**" → hypervigilant

**Therapeutic significance:** The target user is described as someone who "has done the self-help reading" and "can name attachment styles and trauma responses." This means they're likely to use clinical language against themselves — weaponizing therapeutic concepts. "I know I'm being codependent" is not self-awareness — it's self-punishment using borrowed vocabulary.

**Detection note:** Clinical language isn't always echoed — sometimes it's genuine self-understanding. But in the context of a brain dump during overwhelm, it's more likely being used as a weapon than a tool. Flag it and let the investigation step sort it out.

#### 8B: Accusation Language (Someone Else's Words)

Words that sound like things someone said TO the speaker, now said BY the speaker about themselves:

- "**toxic**" → very high signal as self-label
- "**manipulative**" → almost always borrowed
- "**selfish**" → one of the most common borrowed words
- "**dramatic**" → dismissal, usually from a caregiver or partner
- "**overreacting**" → same as dramatic
- "**crazy**" → gendered, often from partners
- "**ungrateful**" → usually parental
- "**lazy**" → can be parental or cultural
- "**sensitive**" → almost always said by someone who wanted the speaker to feel less
- "**high maintenance**" → partner or cultural language
- "**clingy**" → partner language
- "**difficult**" → authority figure language
- "**impossible**" → as in "you're impossible"
- "**exhausting**" → said about the self, originally said BY others about how tiring the speaker is

**Detection heuristic for echoed language:** If the word sounds more like something someone would say about you in frustration than something you'd discover through gentle self-reflection, it's probably echoed. "Manipulative" is not a word people arrive at through compassionate self-inquiry.

#### 8C: Cultural/Media-Borrowed Language

Words absorbed from self-help culture, social media, or popular psychology:

- "**healing**" (when used as pressure: "I should be healing")
- "**growth**" (when obligatory: "I need to grow from this")
- "**boundaries**" (when weaponized: "I can't even maintain boundaries")
- "**self-care**" (when guilt-inducing: "I can't even do self-care right")
- "**toxic positivity**" (when used to dismiss own feelings)
- "**red flags**" (about self: "I AM the red flag")
- "**the work**" (when obligatory: "I haven't done the work")
- "**triggered**" (when used to minimize: "I know I'm just triggered")

### CATEGORY 9: Conflation, Layering & Structural Markers

**What it is:** Not individual words but structural patterns in how sentences are built. These reveal how the speaker's thoughts are tangled — which is the core problem Whelm is designed to address.

**Why it matters:** The brain dump isn't just about which words carry weight — it's about how thoughts are connected. "But," "and," "because," and other connectives can reveal where distinct concerns have been fused into one unsortable mass.

#### 9A: "But" as Conflation Marker

**"But" negates what came before it.** When used between emotional statements, it signals that the speaker can't hold two truths simultaneously.

**Flag "but" when it appears between two emotional or self-evaluative clauses.**

Examples:
- "I know he loves me **but** sometimes I wonder if I'm enough" → but (conflation: love ≠ worthiness)
- "I should be grateful **but** I feel empty" → but (conflation: gratitude ≠ fulfillment)
- "I know it's not rational **but** I can't stop feeling this way" → but (conflation: rationality ≠ validity)
- "I want to leave **but** I'm afraid of being alone" → but (conflation: desire ≠ fear)
- "I know I'm overreacting **but** it really hurt" → but (dismissal + truth in same sentence)

**What to do with flagged "but":** In the word portrait or later processing, "but" can become the place where the app suggests "these might be two separate things." The conflation itself IS the tangle.

**Context sensitivity:** "I wanted pizza but they were closed" = no signal. The "but" must connect emotional or evaluative content.

#### 9B: "And" as Layering/Stacking

**"And" used rapidly between emotional statements signals cascading — one feeling piling on top of another.**

Pattern: `[emotion] and [emotion] and [emotion]`

Examples:
- "I'm **anxious** and **angry** and **ashamed** and I don't know what to do" → Flag each emotion word. The "and" chain is the stacking pattern.
- "I feel **guilty** and **resentful** and **confused**" → Three distinct emotions compressed into one breath.
- "I'm **sad** and **frustrated** and **tired** of being **sad**" → Note the recursion: tired of being sad is meta-emotion.

**What to do with stacking:** Each word in the stack is a candidate for the word portrait. The stack itself tells the user "you're carrying more than one thing." Separation is therapeutic.

#### 9C: "Because" as Justification or Self-Blame

Pattern: `I feel [emotion] because [reason that may be false or borrowed]`

- "I feel this way **because** I'm **broken**" → because + broken (the "because" claims causality that may not be true)
- "I'm upset **because** I can't handle **anything**" → because + can't handle + anything
- "I react like this **because** I'm **damaged**" → because + damaged

**The "because" reveals the user's causal model of their own suffering.** That model is often wrong, borrowed, or overly self-blaming. Flag what comes after "because" — that's where the internalized story lives.

#### 9D: "Even Though" / "Despite" (Self-Contradiction)

Pattern: `even though [positive] I still [negative]`

- "**Even though** I have so much, I still feel **empty**" → empty (the feeling that "shouldn't" exist)
- "**Despite** everything they do for me, I'm still **unhappy**" → unhappy (the forbidden feeling)

**Therapeutic significance:** "Even though" structures reveal guilt about feelings. The speaker is pre-defending against the accusation that they don't have a right to feel what they feel.

#### 9E: "Or" as Confusion / Can't-Sort Signal

Pattern: `[possibility] or [possibility]` in emotional context

- "I don't know if I'm **angry** or **sad**" → angry + sad (can't differentiate)
- "I'm not sure if this is **real** or **anxiety**" → real + anxiety (reality testing)
- "Is this a **boundary** or am I being **controlling**?" → boundary + controlling (self-doubt)

**Therapeutic significance:** "Or" between emotional descriptors signals the user can't sort their experience into distinct feelings. This is exactly what the word portrait is designed to help with.

---

### CATEGORY 10: Catastrophizing & Permanence Language

**What it is:** Words and phrases that project current pain into an endless future or expand a specific situation into total life failure.

**Why it matters:** Catastrophizing is the overwhelm speaking. It's the nervous system's way of saying "this is too much" by making everything too much. The catastrophizing words are the overwhelm's fingerprints.

#### 10A: Permanence Words

- "**forever**" → "I'll feel like this forever"
- "**never going to**" → "I'm never going to get better"
- "**always going to be**" → "I'm always going to be like this"
- "**too late**" → "It's too late to change"
- "**over**" → "It's over" / "My life is over"
- "**done**" → "I'm done" (can signal giving up or healthy boundary — context dependent)
- "**ruined**" → "I've ruined everything"
- "**destroyed**" → "I destroyed it"
- "**wasted**" → "I've wasted my life" / "wasted years"
- "**irreversible**" → "The damage is irreversible"
- "**permanent**" → "This is permanent"
- "**can't go back**" → loss of options

#### 10B: Totality Words

- "**everything**" → "Everything is falling apart"
- "**nothing**" → "Nothing works" / "Nothing matters"
- "**completely**" → "I've completely failed"
- "**totally**" → "I've totally lost it"
- "**entire**" → "My entire life"
- "**whole**" → "My whole world"

---

### CATEGORY 11: Agency & Power Language

**What it is:** Words that reveal the speaker's relationship to their own agency — whether they feel they have choice, power, or control.

**Why it matters:** Loss of agency is central to the overwhelm experience. These words mark where the person feels powerless, and also where they might discover they have more choice than they think.

#### 11A: Agency-Stripping Words

- "**can't**" → (context dependent — see below)
- "**unable**" → "I'm unable to function"
- "**helpless**" → high signal
- "**powerless**" → high signal
- "**out of control**" → loss of agency
- "**no choice**" → perceived loss of options
- "**forced**" → external pressure
- "**trapped**" → no exit perceived
- "**inevitable**" → no agency over outcome

**Critical context rule for "can't":** "Can't" alone is not a signal. "I can't find my keys" = logistical. But:
- "I **can't** do this anymore" → high signal (capacity depletion)
- "I **can't** stop feeling this way" → high signal (loss of control over internal state)
- "I **can't** change" → high signal (permanence + agency loss)
- "I **can't** keep pretending" → high signal (mask fatigue)

The test: Is the "can't" about an external task or about the self/emotions/identity? Self-referential "can't" = signal.

#### 11B: Passive Voice About Own Experience

When the speaker removes themselves as the agent in their own story:

- "It **happened** to me" vs. "I experienced..."
- "I was **made** to feel..." → made (external agency)
- "I was **told** I was..." → told (origin of belief)
- "I got **left**" → left
- "I was **abandoned**" → abandoned

**Note:** Passive voice about traumatic experiences is NOT a problem to flag — it may be the safest way to discuss something. But when passive voice becomes a pattern across non-traumatic experiences, it signals a broader loss of agency.

---

### CATEGORY 12: Meta-Anxiety (Anxiety About The Anxiety)

**What it is:** Language where the speaker is not just feeling something but judging themselves for feeling it. This is the recursive loop that makes overwhelm feel inescapable.

**Why it matters:** Meta-anxiety is what distinguishes "I'm anxious" from "I can't believe I'm anxious about this." The second layer — the judgment about the feeling — is often more painful than the feeling itself. It's also the layer that therapeutic language is most weaponized against.

#### 12A: "I Know" + Self-Dismissal

Pattern: `I know [rational frame] but [emotional truth]`

- "I **know** this is **irrational** but..." → irrational (user pathologizing own feelings)
- "I **know** I **shouldn't** feel this way" → shouldn't (obligation applied to emotions)
- "I **know** it's **not a big deal**" → not a big deal (minimizing)
- "I **know** I'm being **ridiculous**" → ridiculous

**The "I know" is the cognitive self watching the emotional self and judging it.** The word after "I know" + the dismissal word are both signals.

#### 12B: "What's Wrong With Me" Pattern

- "**What's wrong with me**" → wrong (self-pathologizing)
- "**Why am I like this**" → like this (self as problem)
- "**Why do I always** do this" → always + this (pattern self-blame)
- "**What is my problem**" → problem (self as problem)
- "**Something is wrong** with me" → wrong

#### 12C: Self-Monitoring Language

- "I **should** be past this by now" → should + past this (timeline pressure on healing)
- "I **thought** I dealt with this" → dealt with (expectation of resolution)
- "I'm **going backwards**" → backwards (progress narrative applied to healing)
- "I'm not **making progress**" → progress (productivity frame on emotional work)
- "I keep **ending up** back here" → ending up + back (circular pattern frustration)
- "I **thought** I was **better**" → better (healing as linear improvement)

---

### CATEGORY 13: Relational Language (About Others)

**What it is:** How the speaker describes other people in their life, especially when those descriptions reveal the speaker's own wounds.

**Why it matters:** In overwhelm, other people often become part of the tangle. How someone describes another person reveals what dynamic is active. This category is important but sensitive — the app must never position itself as a relationship advisor.

#### 13A: Charged Relational Words

- "**abandoned**" → abandonment wound
- "**betrayed**" → trust violation
- "**rejected**" → rejection sensitivity
- "**dismissed**" → experience of not being heard
- "**invisible**" → not being seen
- "**used**" → exploitation
- "**controlled**" → loss of agency in relationship
- "**suffocated**" → too much closeness
- "**ignored**" → neglect
- "**replaced**" → disposability
- "**blamed**" → scapegoating

#### 13B: Obligation Words About Others

- "I **owe** them" → debt frame in relationship
- "After everything they've **done** for me" → guilt-driven loyalty
- "I **can't** let them down" → can't + let down (external obligation)
- "They **need** me" → need (caretaking identity)
- "I'm **responsible** for their feelings" → responsible (emotional labor)

---

### CATEGORY 14: Silence & Absence Signals

**What it is:** Not what the person says, but what they conspicuously don't say — or say very briefly before moving on.

**Why it matters:** In a brain dump, the places where language becomes sparse, trails off, or is quickly redirected may be the most significant material. These are harder to detect algorithmically but important for AI-assisted parsing.

#### 14A: Trailing Off

- "I don't know if..." (incomplete thought)
- "It's like..." (metaphor started but not finished)
- "I just..." (truncated thought)
- "Never mind" / "Anyway" (redirection away from something)
- "..." (literal ellipsis indicating pause or inability to continue)

#### 14B: Topic Switching Under Emotion

When the user shifts abruptly from an emotional topic to a logistical one:
- "I feel so alone... anyway, I need to figure out the schedule" → The shift IS the signal. Something was too close.

#### 14C: Repetition

**A word or phrase that appears more than twice in a brain dump is high signal regardless of category.**

The user may not even realize they're repeating it. The word portrait surfaces this pattern: "You used the word 'enough' seven times."

## Compound Patterns (Multi-Signal Detection)

Individual signals have varying confidence levels. But when multiple signals co-occur in the same sentence or passage, confidence increases dramatically.

### Compound Pattern Examples

| Raw Text | Signals Detected | Confidence |
|----------|-----------------|------------|
| "I always sabotage everything" | Absolutist (always) + Self-judgment verb (sabotage) + Scope absolutism (everything) | Very High |
| "I'm too sensitive, I should be over this by now" | Intensity marker (too) + Echoed language (sensitive) + Obligation (should) + Timeline pressure (by now) | Very High |
| "I know I'm being dramatic but it feels like everything is falling apart" | Meta-anxiety (I know) + Self-dismissal (dramatic) + Conflation marker (but) + Catastrophizing (everything) + Metaphor (falling apart) | Very High |
| "Why can't I just be normal like everyone else" | Meta-anxiety (why can't I) + Minimizer (just) + Comparison (normal) + Self-exclusion (everyone else) | Very High |
| "I feel behind" | Spatial metaphor (behind) — only one signal | Low-Medium (needs context) |
| "I feel so far behind in my life and I can't catch up" | Intensity (so far) + Spatial metaphor (behind) + Scope (my life) + Agency loss (can't) + Metaphor (catch up) | High |

### The Accumulation Rule

**Within a single passage (3-5 sentences), count the total number of distinct signal categories activated:**

- **1 category** → Low confidence. May flag as candidate, user can confirm/remove.
- **2-3 categories** → Medium confidence. Likely genuine signal.
- **4+ categories** → High confidence. Almost certainly significant material.

### The Repetition Multiplier

**If a word appears 3+ times across the entire brain dump, its confidence level increases by one tier**, regardless of original category signal strength. Someone who keeps returning to "enough" or "wrong" or "too much" is revealing a core concern even if each individual instance is low-signal.

## Context Sensitivity Rules

### Rule 1: Self-Reference Proximity

A word gains signal strength when it's within 5 words of a self-referential pronoun (I, me, my, myself, I'm, I've).

- "The weather is **terrible**" → no signal
- "I feel **terrible**" → signal
- "**Terrible** things keep happening to me" → signal (proximity to "me")

### Rule 2: Emotional Domain vs. Practical Domain

Words in the emotional/identity domain are higher signal than the same words in practical/logistical domain.

- "I **can't** find my phone" → practical, low signal
- "I **can't** find myself" → emotional, high signal
- "I need to **fix** the sink" → practical, no signal
- "I need to **fix** myself" → emotional, high signal

**Detection heuristic:** What is the object of the verb? If the object is the self, a relationship, an emotion, or an identity — it's emotional domain.

### Rule 3: Negation Changes Everything

The same word can be signal or noise depending on negation:

- "I'm **happy**" → low signal (positive self-state)
- "I'm not **happy**" → signal (negation + self-state)
- "I **can't** be **happy**" → higher signal (agency loss + emotional state)
- "I **should** be **happy**" → highest signal (obligation + emotional state)

### Rule 4: Questions vs. Statements

Questions about the self are often higher signal than statements because they reveal uncertainty:

- "Am I **crazy**?" → higher signal than "I'm crazy" (the question form suggests it's someone else's word being tried on)
- "Is this **normal**?" → signal for comparison/standard
- "Am I **overreacting**?" → signal for self-doubt, probably echoed

### Rule 5: Tense Matters

- Present tense ("I am broken") → current identity claim, high signal
- Past tense ("I was broken") → may be narrative, medium signal
- Future tense ("I'll always be broken") → permanence/catastrophizing, very high signal

---

## What We Explicitly Do NOT Flag

### Low-Signal Content to Ignore

- **Proper nouns** (people's names, places, brands) — unless they're being used as metaphor
- **Pure logistics** ("I need to pick up the kids at 3")
- **Physical descriptions of environment** ("It's raining outside")
- **Time/date references** ("Last Tuesday...")
- **Factual reporting without emotional charge** ("He said he'd call back")
- **Filler words** (um, like, you know — unless "you know" is seeking validation)
- **Common pleasantries** used as throat-clearing ("So anyway...")
- **Technical/work jargon** unless emotionally charged ("I missed the deadline" = maybe; "I always miss deadlines" = yes)

### Content to Handle with Extra Care

- **Positive self-affirmations** — Generally don't flag, but consider: is this performative? "I'm fine, I'm strong, I can handle this" during a brain dump may be a mask, not a truth.
- **Humor/sarcasm** — "Great, another thing I'm failing at" — the sarcasm is a signal, but the system needs to recognize the word "great" is inverted.
- **Direct quotes from others** — "She said I'm being unreasonable" — flag "unreasonable" but note it's reported speech, not self-generated.
- **References to specific people** — Handle with privacy awareness. The person's relationship to the named individual matters, but storing others' identities raises ethical concerns.

## Special Pattern: The Spiral Structure

In overwhelm text, there's a common structural pattern that deserves its own attention:

### The Overwhelm Spiral

The speaker starts with a specific trigger, expands to a generalization, then collapses into self-blame:

1. **Trigger:** "I had a fight with my partner"
2. **Expansion:** "We always fight about the same thing"
3. **Generalization:** "I can't maintain any relationship"
4. **Self-blame:** "I'm fundamentally broken"
5. **Meta-anxiety:** "And the fact that I keep doing this proves it"

**Each step in the spiral contains different signal categories:**
1. Factual (low signal)
2. Absolutist (always) — medium signal
3. Catastrophizing (any, can't) — high signal
4. Identity judgment (broken) — very high signal
5. Meta-anxiety (proves) — very high signal

**The spiral structure itself is a pattern to detect.** If the system recognizes an expanding pattern from specific → general → self-judgment, it can surface that pattern: "You started with one specific thing and it grew into a statement about who you are."

## Implementation Approach: Hybrid System

### Layer 1: Heuristic Pattern Matching (Fast, Deterministic)

Rule-based detection for high-confidence patterns:
- "too + [adjective]" near self-reference → flag
- "should" / "shouldn't" + emotional content → flag
- "always" / "never" + self-referential verb → flag
- Direct self-labels ("I'm [charged word]") → flag
- Repetition count (3+) → flag

This layer runs quickly and catches the most obvious signals.

### Layer 2: AI-Assisted Semantic Analysis (Deeper, Contextual)

LLM-based analysis for context-dependent signals:
- Distinguishing "behind the house" from "behind in my life"
- Identifying echoed language vs. self-generated insight
- Detecting sarcasm and inverted meaning
- Recognizing the spiral structure
- Identifying metaphorical vs. literal language
- Detecting trailing off and topic shifts

This layer provides nuance that rules can't capture.

### Layer 3: User Agency (Final Authority)

The user always has the last word:
- Words the system flagged that don't resonate can be dismissed
- Words the system missed can be highlighted by the user
- The user can add context the system couldn't detect
- Nothing is final until the user confirms it

**This layer is not optional.** It's the difference between an AI telling someone what their words mean and a mirror showing them what they said.

### Priority Order for Flagging

When the system has limited capacity (short brain dump, processing constraints), prioritize detection in this order:

1. **Self-judgment identity statements** (Category 1) — highest therapeutic significance
2. **"Too" / intensity patterns** (Category 2) — almost always echoed language
3. **Obligation/should patterns** (Category 4) — reveals external authority
4. **Conflation markers** (Category 9) — reveals where things are tangled
5. **Metaphorical/embodied language** (Category 6) — body speaking through words
6. **Absolutist language** (Category 3) — overwhelm fingerprint
7. **Meta-anxiety** (Category 12) — the recursive trap
8. **Minimizing/dismissal** (Category 5) — pre-emptive self-silencing
9. **Echoed/borrowed language** (Category 8) — not-my-words detection
10. **Everything else** — as capacity allows

## Ethical Guardrails for the Parser

### 1. Surface, Don't Interpret

The parser identifies what carries weight. It does NOT determine what the weight means. "You used the word 'toxic' five times" is surfacing. "You're being too hard on yourself" is interpreting.

### 2. No Pathologizing

The parser never labels patterns as symptoms, disorders, or diagnoses. "Catastrophizing" is useful as an internal category name but should NEVER be shown to the user. The user-facing equivalent is "growing bigger than the moment."

### 3. The Void Contract Applies

If the brain dump promised that narrative falls away and words stay, the parser must honor that. No full sentences are stored or displayed. Only the distilled words/phrases. The user must see this in action — words lingering as sentences dissolve — so they know the contract is being kept.

### 4. No Surveillance

The parser does not track patterns across sessions without explicit user consent and understanding. If longitudinal pattern recognition is added later, the user must opt in and understand exactly what is being tracked.

### 5. Err Toward Under-Detection

It is better to miss a signal than to surface something that doesn't resonate. A false positive in this context means showing someone a word they don't connect with — which undermines trust in the mirror. A false negative means missing something — which the user can catch themselves or which may surface next time.

### 6. Crisis Detection Is Separate

If the parser detects language indicating acute crisis (self-harm, suicidal ideation), this triggers the crisis boundary protocol, NOT the word portrait flow. Crisis language is handled by safety.md rules, not by the word parser.

**Specific crisis markers (route to safety protocol, do not process as word portrait material):**
- Direct statements of self-harm intent
- Specific methods or plans
- "I want to die" / "I don't want to be here anymore"
- "No one would notice if I was gone"
- Expressions of hopelessness that include finality

## The Whelm Vocabulary Bridge

For words that are flagged, the system should be aware of Whelm's own non-clinical vocabulary for common patterns. This vocabulary does not get shown DURING the brain dump — it's for later, if the user enters investigation. But the parser should be mapping to these categories internally:

| Pattern | Clinical Term | Whelm Vocabulary | Description |
|---------|--------------|-----------------|-------------|
| Fusing separate concerns into one | Conflating / cognitive fusion | **Tangled together** | Two or more things that feel like one but are actually separate |
| One moment becoming everything | Overgeneralizing | **Growing bigger than the moment** | A thought that expanded beyond what actually happened |
| Multiple assumptions in one thought | Bundling | **Layered** / **Carrying a lot inside it** | A thought with hidden questions or assumptions underneath |
| Using someone else's language about yourself | Internalized narrative | **Echoing** | Words or beliefs borrowed from someone else, not originally yours |
| Feelings compounding on top of each other | Emotional cascading | **Stacking** | One feeling piling on top of another |
| Judging yourself for your own feelings | Meta-emotion | **Watching yourself feel** | The layer above the feeling that says it's wrong to feel it |
| Describing permanent identity from temporary state | Cognitive fusion with self-concept | **Wearing it as a name** | Turning a moment into who you are |
| Using clinical language as self-punishment | Weaponized therapeutic literacy | **Knowledge without kindness** | Using what you've learned about yourself as ammunition |

## Testing This Ruleset

### Test Passage 1 (From Framework Case Study)

> "I had a hypersensitive moment and I'm feeling shame and embarrassed and I keep doing this toxic thing and I feel damaged and I'm probably sabotaging everything."

**Expected detection:**
- "hypersensitive" → echoed/borrowed (Category 8) — probably someone else's word
- "shame" → emotion word, medium signal
- "embarrassed" → emotion word, medium signal  
- "and...and...and" → stacking pattern (Category 9B)
- "keep doing" → verb as identity pattern (Category 1B)
- "toxic" → very high signal — echoed accusation language (Category 8B)
- "damaged" → identity statement (Category 1A) + echoed language (Category 8)
- "probably" → hedging that reveals self-doubt
- "sabotaging" → verb as identity (Category 1B) + echoed (Category 8)
- "everything" → scope absolutism (Category 3B)

**Compound signal:** This single sentence activates Categories 1, 3, 8, and 9 — very high confidence passage.

### Test Passage 2

> "I feel so behind in my life. Everyone else seems to have it figured out and I'm just stuck. I should be further along by now. What's wrong with me?"

**Expected detection:**
- "so" → intensifier (Category 2B)
- "behind" → spatial metaphor + self-referential (Category 6A) — context makes it high signal
- "in my life" → scope marker that elevates "behind"
- "everyone else" → self-exclusion (Category 7C)
- "figured out" → the standard being failed
- "just" → minimizer (Category 5A)
- "stuck" → spatial metaphor (Category 6A)
- "should" → obligation (Category 4A)
- "further along" → timeline/progress narrative
- "by now" → timeline pressure (Category 4A extension)
- "what's wrong with me" → meta-anxiety, self-pathologizing (Category 12B)

### Test Passage 3

> "I know this is irrational but I feel like I can't breathe whenever he doesn't text back. I'm being clingy. A normal person wouldn't care this much. I'm too much."

**Expected detection:**
- "I know" → meta-anxiety prefix (Category 12A)
- "irrational" → self-pathologizing (Category 12A)
- "but" → conflation marker (Category 9A)
- "can't breathe" → embodied metaphor (Category 6B/6E), very high signal
- "clingy" → echoed/accusation language (Category 8B)
- "normal person" → comparison/self-exclusion (Category 7C)
- "wouldn't care this much" → implied "too much" (Category 2)
- "too much" → intensity marker (Category 2A), identity statement (Category 1A), possibly the core wound

### Test Passage 4 (Low-Signal)

> "I need to pick up groceries and also call the dentist. My car needs an oil change. I think it's supposed to rain tomorrow."

**Expected detection:** Nothing. This is logistical content. No self-referential judgment, no emotional charge, no metaphorical language about internal state. The parser should return nothing.

## Living Document Notice

This ruleset is a starting point, not an endpoint. It should be:

1. **Tested against real overwhelm language** — collected (with consent) from friends, users, and the developer's own experience
2. **Refined through use** — false positives and false negatives should be tracked
3. **Grown from actual user brain dumps** — "the words people actually use when falling apart are more honest than anything a designer would invent"
4. **Culturally expanded** — different cultural contexts will have different charged words, different echoed language, different metaphorical systems

The principles are stable. The specific word lists are emerging. That balance is intentional.
